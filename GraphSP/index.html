Graph Signals Definition

A graph is defined as $$\mathcal{G} = \{ \mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, W\} $$

  • $\mathcal{V}$: Set of vertices
  • $\mathcal{E}$: Set of edges, where an edge is defined as $e=(i,j)$, which connects vertices $i$ and $j$
  • $W$: Adjacency matrix $$ W_{i,j} = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if $i=j$ (no self-loop is assumed)}\\ \text{edge weight of $(i,j)$} & \text{if $i\neq j$ and $v_i$, $v_j$ are connected} \\ 0 & \text{o.w.} \end{cases} $$

A graph signal is defined as $$f : \mathcal{V} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$$ $$\textbf{f} \in \mathbb{R}^N$$ with a graph $\mathcal{G} = \{ \mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, W\} $

Finite-Length DT Signals Definition

A finite-length signal is defined as $$f : \mathbb{Z} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$$ $$\textbf{f} \in \mathbb{R}^N$$ with a graph $\mathcal{G} = \{ \mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}, W\}$ with $W_{i,j} = 1$ if $i=j+1$ or $ i+1=j$, otherwise it's equal to $0$.

So, a finite-length signal can be seen as a graph signal where the graph is a line with the same edge weight that represents the regularly spaced vertices.

Graph Laplacian Operator

Graph Laplacian operator is defined as: $$ Ł : \mathbb{R}^N \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^N$$ $$ (Łf)(i) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{R}_i} W_{i,j}[f(i)-f(j)] $$ where $\mathcal{R}_i$ is neighborhood of vertex $i$, i.e. the set of vertices that have an edge with vertex $i$.

Note:

Graph Laplacian operator can be represented by a matrix:

$$ Ł = D - W $$ $$ Ł_{i,j} = \begin{cases} d_i & \text{if $i=j$} \\ -W_{i,j} & \text{if $i\neq j$ and $v_i$, $v_j$ are connected} \end{cases} $$ where $D$ is the degree matrix of a graph, which is a diagonal matrix with $i^{th}$ entry, $d_i = \sum_{j=1, \: i\neq j }^N W_{i,j}$

Laplacian Operator

Laplacian operator is defined as: $$\Delta = \nabla^2 = \nabla . \nabla $$ So, Laplacian operator is divergence of gradient.

Note:

Given that function $f$ is dependent on parameters $\{x_i\}^N_{i=0}$
  • Gradient: $$\nabla f = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} & ... & \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_N} \end{bmatrix}^T$$
  • Divergence: $$ \nabla . v = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial}{\partial x_1} & ... & \frac{\partial}{\partial x_N} \end{bmatrix}^T . \begin{bmatrix} v_1 & ... & v_N \end{bmatrix}^T = \sum_{i=1}^N \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial x_i} $$
  • Laplacian: $$ \Delta f = \sum_{i=1}^N \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i^2} $$
Graph Fourier Transform

Graph Fourier transform is defined as: $$ \hat{f}(\lambda_l) = < f, u_l > = \sum_{i=1}^N f(i) u_l(i) $$ where $\{\lambda_l\}_{l=1}^N$ and $\{u_l\}_{l=1}^N$ are eigenvalues and corresponding eigenvectors of graph Laplacian operators, i.e. $$ Ł u_l = \lambda_l u_l$$ Inverse graph Fourier transform is defined as: $$ f(i) = \sum_{l=0}^{N-1}\hat{f}(\lambda_l)u_l(i) $$

Note:

Assume eigenvalues of graph Laplacian are ordered: $$ 0 = \lambda_0 \leq \lambda_1 \leq ... \leq \lambda_n = \lambda_{max} $$ These eigenvalues have the notion of frequency. The higher the eigenvalue is, more rapidly the corresponding eigenvector oscillates. For example, eigenvector $u_0$ corresponding to the eigenvalue $\lambda_0=0$ is equal to constant vector of which each element is equal to $1/\sqrt{N}$.

Note:

Graph signals which have the signal representation both in vertex and spectral domain are called kernels.

Heat Kernel:

Heat kernel is defined directly in the graph spectral domain as $\hat{g}(\lambda_l) = e^{-5\lambda_l}$. Then, we should apply inverse graph Fourier transform to obtain $g(i)$.
Fourier Transform

Fourier transform is defined as: $$\hat{x}(f) = \int x(t) e^{-j2\pi ft}dt = < x(t), e^{j2\pi ft} >$$ Inverse Fourier transform is defined as: $$ x(t) = \int \hat{x}(f) e^{j2\pi ft} df $$

Note:

Fourier transform is obtained by an inner product of the signal with eigenfunctions of Laplacian operator: $$ -\Delta (e^{j2\pi ft}) = \frac{\partial^2 (e^{j2\pi ft})}{\partial t^2} = (2\pi f)^2 (e^{j2\pi ft}) $$
  • Eigenfunction: $e^{j2\pi ft}$
  • Eigenvalue: $(2\pi f)^2$, which has a notion of frequency. For large values of $f$, the corresponding eigenfunction oscillates more rapidly .
Signal Smoothness of Graph Signals

Smoothnesses are with respect to the intrinsic structure of the data domain - weighted graph.

  • Edge derivative of $f$ wrt edge $e=(i,j)$ at vertex $i$: $$ \left.\frac{\partial f}{\partial e}\right\vert_i = \sqrt{W_{i,j}} [f(j)-f(i)] $$
  • Graph gradient of $f$ at vertex $i$: $$ \nabla_i f = \begin{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \left.\frac{\partial f}{\partial e}\right\vert_i \end{Bmatrix}_{e \in \mathcal{E} \: \text{for some} \: j \in \mathcal{V}} \end{bmatrix} $$
  • Local variation of $f$ at vertex $i$: $$ ||\nabla_i f||_2 = \begin{bmatrix} \sum_{e \in \mathcal{E} \: \text{s.t.} \: e=(i,j) \: \text{for some} \: j \in \mathcal{V}} (\left.\frac{\partial f}{\partial e}\right\vert_i)^2 \end{bmatrix}^{\frac{1}{2}} $$ $$ ||\nabla_i f||_2 = \begin{bmatrix} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_i} W_{i,j}[f(j) - f(i)]^2 \end{bmatrix}^{\frac{1}{2}} $$
  • Discrete p-Dirichlet form of $f$: $$ S_p (f) = \frac{1}{p} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{V}} ||\nabla_i f||_2^p = \frac{1}{p} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{V}}[\sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_i} W_{i,j} [f(j)-f(i)]^2]^{\frac{p}{2}} $$

Note:

  • $S_1(f)$ is the total variation of the signal with respect to the graph
  • $S_2(f)= \textbf{f}^T Ł \textbf{f}$ is the graph Laplacian quadratic form.
  • Semi norm, $||\textbf{f}||_Ł = ||Ł^{\frac{1}{2}}\textbf{f}||_2 = \sqrt{\textbf{f}^T Ł \textbf{f}} = \sqrt{S_2 (f)}$
Fourier Transform

Fourier transform is defined as: $$\hat{x}(f) = \int x(t) e^{-j2\pi ft}dt = < x(t), e^{j2\pi ft} >$$ Inverse Fourier transform is defined as: $$ x(t) = \int \hat{x}(f) e^{j2\pi ft} df $$

Note:

Fourier transform is obtained by an inner product of the signal with eigenfunctions of Laplacian operator: $$ -\Delta (e^{j2\pi ft}) = \frac{\partial^2 (e^{j2\pi ft})}{\partial t^2} = (2\pi f)^2 (e^{j2\pi ft}) $$
  • Eigenfunction: $e^{j2\pi ft}$
  • Eigenvalue: $(2\pi f)^2$, which has a notion of frequency. For large values of $f$, the corresponding eigenfunction oscillates more rapidly .
